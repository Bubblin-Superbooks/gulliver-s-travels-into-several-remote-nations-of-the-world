Above it lie the several minerals in their usual order, and over all is a coat of rich mould, ten or twelve feet deep.  The declivity of the upper surface, from the circumference to the centre, is the natural cause why all the dews and rains, which fall upon the island, are conveyed in small rivulets toward the middle, where they are emptied into four large basins, each of about half a mile in circuit, and two hundred yards distant from the centre.  From these basins the water is continually exhaled by the sun in the daytime, which effectually prevents their overflowing.  Besides, as it is in the power of the monarch to raise the island above the region of clouds and vapours, he can prevent the falling of dews and rain whenever he pleases.  For the highest clouds cannot rise above two miles, as naturalists agree, at least they were never known to do so in that country.
